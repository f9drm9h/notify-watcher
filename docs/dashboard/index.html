60The discovery of Uranus by William HerschelFor millennia, the night sky presented humanity with a predictable, finite cosmos. Six planets, Mercury to Saturn, traced their familiar paths, their existence known since antiquity, their celestial dance charting the rhythm of time and inspiring the earliest mythologies. This was the established order, a cosmic blueprint that had remained unchallenged across cultures and civilizations. The idea that there might be *more* was, for the most part, not even a question posed, let alone answered. Telescopes, developed centuries earlier, had unveiled moons and rings, features of these known worlds, but the fundamental architecture of the solar system seemed immutable. Into this fixed firmament stepped William Herschel, a man of profound musical talent who, in his late thirties, became utterly captivated by the heavens. He wasn't looking for a new planet; he was driven by an insatiable curiosity, a desire to peer deeper into the universe than anyone before him, meticulously grinding his own mirrors to achieve unprecedented clarity and magnification. The night of March 13, 1781, was, to begin with, no different from countless others Herschel had spent at his telescope in Bath, England, with his devoted sister Caroline often assisting. His method was systematic: a painstaking survey of the sky, star by star, cataloging and noting anything unusual. On this particular evening, his powerful seven-foot reflecting telescope, a marvel of his own craftsmanship, brought into view a faint, greenish object in the constellation of Gemini. It wasn't sparkling like a star, nor was it a fuzzy nebula. It had a discernible disc, a peculiar characteristic that immediately piqued his scientific intuition. Initially, Herschel, ever the meticulous observer, suspected it might be a comet, a common enough celestial visitor. He recorded its position and continued his observations over the following nights. The astronomical community, particularly in continental Europe, favored a name rooted in classical mythology, in keeping with the other planets. Johann Elert Bode, a German astronomer, suggested "Uranus," the Greek god of the sky and father of Saturn, thus preserving the mythological lineage. The discovery profoundly altered humanity's understanding of its place in the cosmos. 60The War of the Currents: Edison versus WestinghouseThe air in the late 19th century crackled with a new kind of energy, a promise of light and power that would forever transform the human experience. At the forefront of this nascent electrical revolution stood two titans, men whose names would become synonymous with progress and, for a time, bitter rivalry: Thomas Edison and George Westinghouse. Edison, the Wizard of Menlo Park, had already captivated the world with his incandescent light bulb, a delicate glass sphere that banished the flickering gaslights of old. He championed direct current, or DC, a steady, unidirectional flow of electricity that had its undeniable advantages in simplicity and direct application for early electrical devices. Westinghouse, a visionary engineer and industrialist, arrived on the scene with a different proposition, one that would spark a heated and often venomous debate: alternating current, or AC. The fundamental disagreement between these two giants stemmed from a basic principle of physics: how to efficiently transmit electricity over long distances. Edison’s DC system was limited by voltage drop; power plants needed to be located every mile or so to maintain sufficient voltage for homes and businesses. This meant a sprawling, inefficient network of power stations, each serving a relatively small radius. Westinghouse, however, saw the potential of AC, which could be easily transformed to higher voltages for long-distance transmission and then stepped down again for local use. This dramatically reduced the number of power plants required and opened up the possibility of electrifying entire cities, even rural areas, from a central generating station. The inherent efficiency and scalability of AC were clear to Westinghouse and his brilliant engineer, Nikola Tesla, who had developed the groundbreaking AC induction motor. What began as a technical debate soon devolved into a full-blown propaganda war, a public spectacle designed to sway opinion and undermine the opposition. Edison, a master of public relations and a fierce protector of his inventions, launched a relentless smear campaign against AC. He painted it as inherently dangerous, a lethal force that would electrocute anyone who came into contact with it. He funded grisly demonstrations where animals – dogs, cats, even a circus elephant named Topsy – were publicly electrocuted with AC to illustrate its supposed deadly nature. He even lobbied for the first electric chair to use AC, hoping to indelibly link Westinghouse's current with capital punishment. This was a dark chapter in Edison's otherwise brilliant career, a testament to the lengths to which commercial rivalry could push even the most revered innovators. Despite Edison's desperate attempts to discredit it, alternating current ultimately prevailed. The advantages of AC in terms of efficiency, cost-effectiveness, and scalability proved too compelling to ignore. The World's Columbian Exposition in Chicago in 1893, famously "lit up" by Westinghouse, served as a stunning demonstration of AC's power and potential, showcasing a dazzling spectacle of light that captivated millions.

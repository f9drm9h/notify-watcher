60Riemann and the geometry of curved spaceIn the mid-19th century, mathematics, particularly geometry, seemed a settled discipline. For two millennia, since the time of Euclid, the axioms and theorems of geometry had stood as an unassailable edifice of pure reason, a reflection of the very structure of the physical universe. Parallel lines, everyone knew, never met. The sum of angles in a triangle was always 180 degrees. Space was flat, infinite, and immutable. This was the bedrock of our understanding of measurement, surveying, and even astronomy. But cracks had begun to appear in this ancient foundation, whispered doubts about Euclid's fifth postulate – the parallel postulate – leading to the strange, beautiful, and ultimately world-shattering non-Euclidean geometries of Bolyai and Lobachevsky, where parallel lines could diverge, and triangles could have angle sums less than 180 degrees. These were intellectual curiosities, fascinating thought experiments, but they lacked a unifying framework, a way to connect these seemingly disparate geometries and understand their potential physical relevance. Enter Bernhard Riemann, a frail and often sickly young man from a small German village, possessed of an intellect that burned with an almost otherworldly intensity. He laid the mathematical groundwork for understanding spaces of any number of dimensions, with any conceivable curvature. It was a purely abstract, mathematical endeavor, with no immediate experimental verification or practical application. Indeed, it would be decades before its true significance would be recognized outside of esoteric mathematical circles. The profound implications of Riemann's work would finally burst forth onto the world stage some sixty years later, when Albert Einstein, grappling with the nature of gravity, found himself stymied by the limitations of traditional physics.

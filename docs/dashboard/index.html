60The War of the Currents: Edison versus WestinghouseThe air in the late 19th century crackled with a new kind of energy, a promise of light and power that would forever transform the human experience. At the forefront of this nascent electrical revolution stood two titans, men whose names would become synonymous with progress and, for a time, bitter rivalry: Thomas Edison and George Westinghouse. Edison, the Wizard of Menlo Park, had already captivated the world with his incandescent light bulb, a delicate glass sphere that banished the flickering gaslights of old. He championed direct current, or DC, a steady, unidirectional flow of electricity that had its undeniable advantages in simplicity and direct application for early electrical devices. Westinghouse, a visionary engineer and industrialist, arrived on the scene with a different proposition, one that would spark a heated and often venomous debate: alternating current, or AC. The fundamental disagreement between these two giants stemmed from a basic principle of physics: how to efficiently transmit electricity over long distances. Edison’s DC system was limited by voltage drop; power plants needed to be located every mile or so to maintain sufficient voltage for homes and businesses. This meant a sprawling, inefficient network of power stations, each serving a relatively small radius. Westinghouse, however, saw the potential of AC, which could be easily transformed to higher voltages for long-distance transmission and then stepped down again for local use. This dramatically reduced the number of power plants required and opened up the possibility of electrifying entire cities, even rural areas, from a central generating station. The inherent efficiency and scalability of AC were clear to Westinghouse and his brilliant engineer, Nikola Tesla, who had developed the groundbreaking AC induction motor. What began as a technical debate soon devolved into a full-blown propaganda war, a public spectacle designed to sway opinion and undermine the opposition. Edison, a master of public relations and a fierce protector of his inventions, launched a relentless smear campaign against AC. He painted it as inherently dangerous, a lethal force that would electrocute anyone who came into contact with it. He funded grisly demonstrations where animals – dogs, cats, even a circus elephant named Topsy – were publicly electrocuted with AC to illustrate its supposed deadly nature. He even lobbied for the first electric chair to use AC, hoping to indelibly link Westinghouse's current with capital punishment. This was a dark chapter in Edison's otherwise brilliant career, a testament to the lengths to which commercial rivalry could push even the most revered innovators. Despite Edison's desperate attempts to discredit it, alternating current ultimately prevailed. The advantages of AC in terms of efficiency, cost-effectiveness, and scalability proved too compelling to ignore. The World's Columbian Exposition in Chicago in 1893, famously "lit up" by Westinghouse, served as a stunning demonstration of AC's power and potential, showcasing a dazzling spectacle of light that captivated millions. The Niagara Falls hydroelectric project, completed a few years later, further cemented AC's dominance, proving its ability to harness immense power and transmit it over vast distances.
